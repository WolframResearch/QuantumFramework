# Was a novel WL-enabled approach missed? A skeptical re-examination

## 0. What this note settles

A prior note, `pathsum-build/PathSum-WL-Note.md`, concluded that the Wolfram Language path-sum work is "an *implementation and an exposition*", not a method: the path-sum object, the rewrite rules, the residual completion, the hidden-shift application, and the confluence result are all prior art (Feynman; Dawson et al. 2004; Montanaro 2016; Koh-Penney-Spekkens 2017; Amy 2018; Amy-Stinchcombe 2024), and "a Wolfram Language version re-derives none of this mathematics." The user suspects this verdict prematurely buried a genuinely novel idea that an earlier reading of 91 Wolfram Language documentation pages was meant to seed.

I re-read all of it with fresh eyes and the opposite question. The earlier mapping, `WL-Symbolic-Toolkit-for-PathSums.md`, asked of each function "is it Core / Supporting / Tangential / Unrelated **relative to the path-sum pipeline**", that is, *which primitive implements which existing step*. I asked instead: *what can these primitives do together that the competing tools cannot do at all?* I checked every candidate against the fetched arXiv TeX, and against the live kernel via `wolframscript`.

> **Bottom line, stated once and up front.** The harsh half of the prior verdict is correct: **no candidate breaks the $\#\mathrm{P}$ wall and none extends the asymptotically tractable class beyond what Amy, Montanaro, and de Colnet already reach.** There is no new complexity-theoretic method here. But the verdict was too flat in one specific place. **One capability is genuinely unique to a computer-algebra host and is possessed by none of the competing simulators: exact path sums with free-symbol gate parameters, producing closed-form amplitude and expectation *functions* over a whole parametrized circuit family in a single pass.** The prior note saw this (its Section 4.3) but filed it under "implementation"; it is better described as a capability no other tool has. A second direction, the Gröbner-basis / variety point count, is a genuine *realization* of a problem that Amy-Stinchcombe explicitly leave open, distinct in method from every cited simulator, but it is bounded to the $\mathbb{F}_2$ (real-amplitude) fragment and, by the originators' own words, "offloads the difficulty to the similarly difficult problem of computing Gröbner bases and counting points in varieties." The remaining two directions ($GF(2^k)$ character sums, holonomic/generating-function tricks) are dead ends, blocked by a clean characteristic-2 obstruction that I verify below.

So the user is **partly right**: a real, under-recognized, WL-unique *capability* was undersold, and a distinct *algorithm* was filed as "future work" without noting it is a different tractability axis. The user is **partly wrong**: neither is a new method that enlarges the efficiently simulable class, and the prior note's central claim ("no new mathematics") stands.

## 1. The capability inventory: what the 91 pages JOINTLY expose

The 91 pages come from the [FiniteFields](https://reference.wolfram.com/language/guide/FiniteFields.html) and [PolynomialAlgebra](https://reference.wolfram.com/language/guide/PolynomialAlgebra.html) guides, the `Hold*` evaluation-control family, `NonThreadable`, and the `Inactive` family. Read for *joint* capability rather than per-step role, they expose four things that a Haskell toolkit (`feynman`), a Rust ZX tool (`quizx`), or a BDD / weighted-model-counting / #SAT engine (de Colnet et al.) structurally do not have.

**C1. Free-symbol algebra is the default, not a bolt-on.** Every solver and every polynomial operation carries symbolic parameters natively. `Coefficient`, `Collect`, `CoefficientRules` split a phase polynomial $\varphi = \varphi_0 + x_v\,C_v$ whether or not the coefficients are numbers; `Root` carries parametric algebraic numbers (`Root[-1+a^2-2 a\,\#1+\#1^5\&,1]`) and is `Series`- and `AsymptoticSolve`-expandable in the parameter; `RootReduce` canonicalizes only parameter-free roots (a real limit, noted below). The competing tools are all built on *fixed numeric coefficient rings*: `feynman` works in the dyadic ring $\mathbb{D}[\omega]$ with concrete gate phases, `quizx` in $\mathbb{D}[e^{i\pi/4}]$, de Colnet over $\mathbb{Z}_r$ with numeric weights. A grep of all thirteen fetched papers for "symbolic parameter", "free angle", "parametric" returns **nothing**: the path-sum literature is "symbolic" only in the *path variables*, never in the *gate angles*. This is the substrate for candidate (b).

**C2. The Boolean-ideal / variety machinery is one switch away.** `Modulus -> p` turns `Solve`, `Reduce`, `Factor`, `GroebnerBasis`, `PolynomialReduce`, `Resultant`, `MatrixRank`, `RowReduce`, `NullSpace`, `LinearSolve`, `Inverse`, `Det` into $\mathbb{F}_p$ operations; the native `FiniteField[2,k]` is $GF(2^k)$ with exact arithmetic and atomic elements carrying `"Coefficients"` bit vectors. `GroebnerBasis[polys \cup \{x_i^2-x_i\}, vars, \text{Modulus} \to 2]` is the radical Boolean ideal; by Hilbert's Nullstellensatz the basis is $\{1\}$ exactly when there is no common zero. For *linear* polynomials `GroebnerBasis` is Gaussian elimination, and `PolynomialReduce` gives unique normal forms against it. `Reduce`, `FindInstance`, and `Resolve` decide and project over `FiniteField` and `Booleans` domains, including characteristic 2. This is the substrate for candidate (a). The crucial gap, which I make precise below, is that none of these primitives is a *weighted point count*: they decide consistency, project, enumerate, or do linear algebra, but the path-sum amplitude is $\sum_v \omega^{\varphi(v)}$, a count of variety points *weighted by a $\mathbb{Z}_8$ phase*, and that has no native primitive.

**C3. Native finite-field traces, norms, Frobenius, and subfield towers.** `FiniteFieldElementTrace` gives the absolute trace $\mathrm{Tr}(a)=a+a^2+\cdots+a^{2^{k-1}}\in\mathbb{F}_2$, and every $\mathbb{F}_2$-linear functional is of this form, so $(-1)^{\mathrm{Tr}(a)}$ is the additive character of $GF(2^k)$. `FrobeniusAutomorphism`, `FiniteFieldEmbedding` (with relative traces and a `"Projection"`), and `MinimalPolynomial` over a subfield give the full Galois apparatus. This is the substrate for candidate (c).

**C4. Exact cyclotomic arithmetic and a native term-rewriting engine.** `Cyclotomic[8,x] = 1+x^4` is $\omega$'s minimal polynomial; `Root`/`RootReduce` canonicalize any element of $\mathbb{Z}[\omega,1/\sqrt2]$ to a single comparable algebraic number with no floating point and no integer overflow; `Extension`/`GaussianIntegers` set the coefficient field; `NHold*` keep $\omega$ and $\sqrt2$ exact under `N`. And the `Inactive[Sum]` + `HoldPattern` + `Activate` triad lets a formal sum be *carried, differentiated, and rewritten without being expanded* (the docs even show `Inactive[Sum]` is faster than `Sum` because it never expands), with `HoldAll` and `NonThreadable` marking a custom path-sum head a single non-scalar atom. In Wolfram Language the rewriter *is* the language: Amy hand-codes a term-rewriting engine in Haskell, whereas `Inactive[Sum]` + pattern rules + `Activate` is the same engine, native. This is the substrate for candidate (d).

These four are real, and they are real *gaps* in the competitors. The question is whether any of them is a *new method* or merely a more comfortable home for an old one.

## 2. The framing diagnosis: why the prior reading filed these as "implementation"

The earlier mapping was explicitly organized around the existing pipeline. Its own header: "Every function below is read in full and tagged **Core / Supporting / Tangential / Unrelated relative to the path-sum pipeline**." Its payoff section lists the upgrades as *implementation cleanups of an existing step*:

> "1. **Replace the hand-rolled Boolean reduction** with `GroebnerBasis`/`PolynomialReduce` against the ideal $(x_i^2 - x_i)$ at `Modulus -> 2` ... This is *also* the literal realization of the report's named 'sum over varieties / ideals' generalization."

That sentence does both things at once: it correctly identifies the Gröbner direction *and* frames it as "replace the hand-rolled reduction", an implementation swap, with the genuinely-new-generalization reading as an aside. The verdict note inherits the framing. Its abstract calls the whole effort "an *implementation and an exposition* ... not a method." Its contribution table tags "Symbolic gate angles" and "Native integration" as **implementation**. And it files the variety direction under future work:

> Section 5: "... with a `Modulus -> 2` Gröbner/variety path (the generalization Amy-Stinchcombe leave open) sitting one function call away."
> Section 7: "The open directions are all known: ... realize the variety/Gröbner-basis generalization with `GroebnerBasis[..., Modulus -> 2]` ..."

The note *did* locally recognize the symbolic-angle capability ("This is a capability difference, not merely a speed ratio: the dense path has no symbolic-$\theta$ amplitude at all", Section 4.3). But it then globally classified it as "implementation" because the organizing question was always *"is this a new method?"* (answer: no) and never *"is this a capability no other tool has?"* (answer, for symbolic parameters: yes). The companion `Beyond-SymPhase-Symbolic-Algebra-For-Magic.md` makes the same move: "Porting it to Wolfram Language, using native $\mathbb{F}_2$ polynomial reduction and `GroebnerBasis` for the variety-valued generalization, is the natural build" (Section 4b), the natural *build*, not a distinct method.

So the diagnosis is precise: **the survey was framed as "which primitive implements each existing step", which is exactly why the two real findings were recorded as an implementation swap and a future-work item rather than as (i) a capability no competitor has and (ii) a distinct algorithm with a different tractability parameter.** Nothing was unseen; two things were mis-weighted.

## 3. Per-candidate skeptical verdicts

For each candidate I give the literature anchor, the kernel check (all verified by `wolframscript`, script `novelty-demos.wls` in this folder), and a one-line verdict.

### (a) The Gröbner-basis / variety point count over $\mathbb{F}_2$

**The idea, and exactly whose it is.** For the Toffoli-Hadamard fragment (gate set $\{H, Z, CZ, CCZ\}$, a *real* phase $(-1)^{f}$), Montanaro's correctness proof gives, verbatim from `1607.08473/lowdegree.tex`:
$$
\langle 0|C|0\rangle = \frac{\mathrm{gap}(f_C)}{2^{h/2+\ell}},\qquad
\mathrm{gap}(f) = \sum_{x\in\{0,1\}^n}(-1)^{f(x)} = \#\{x: f(x)=0\} - \#\{x: f(x)=1\}.
$$
The amplitude *is* a signed point count of the $\mathbb{F}_2$ variety $\{f=0\}$. This is not Amy-Stinchcombe's idea and not the prior note's idea: it is in the **original 2004 Dawson paper**, which states it in so many words (`quant-ph_0408129/paper_final.tex`): "computation is equivalent to counting the number of solutions to ... this means **counting the number of points in an algebraic variety**", and again "the problem of **counting the points in an algebraic variety** ... is a well-known problem in mathematics." What Amy-Stinchcombe name as *future work* is the narrower computational technique of absorbing the *whole phase polynomial into an ideal* and solving by Gröbner machinery (`2408.02778/sections/conclusion.tex`):

> "One potential avenue ... alluded to in [Amy 2023] is to generalize the sum from variables to *varieties* over $\mathbb{F}_2^k$, which allows the entire phase polynomial to be deterministically absorbed into an ideal of polynomial equations. **In one sense this offloads the difficulty of simulation to the similarly difficult problem of computing Gröbner bases and counting points in varieties**, and so it remains a question for future work to explore this technique."

The originators flag, in the very sentence that proposes it, that this is *not* a speedup: it trades the path sum for two problems (Gröbner basis computation, which is doubly-exponential in the worst case, and point counting, which is $\#\mathrm{P}$-hard in the worst case) of comparable hardness.

**Is it distinct from the other simulators?** Yes. de Colnet et al. (`2605.29944/main.tex`) evaluate the *same* sum-of-powers object (citing Dawson, not Amy) by a **rank-width fixed-parameter dynamic program**, equivalently graphical-model bucket elimination, dispatched onto BDD and weighted-model-counting engines: time $2^{O(k)}\mathrm{poly}(n)$ in the rank-width $k$ of the path-variable interaction graph. That is a *different tractability parameter* (rank-width of a graph) from Gröbner-basis complexity (regularity of an ideal). `quizx`'s "variety" hits are false positives ("a variety of applications", "$Z$/$X$ spider varieties"); it does no ideal arithmetic. So the Gröbner-variety realization is genuinely un-built in the cited literature, and it is *not* subsumed by de Colnet.

**Kernel check (verified).** The variety machinery is exactly as native as claimed. The Nullstellensatz amplitude-zero test is one call: an inconsistent system gives `GroebnerBasis[{x+y,x,y+1},{x,y},Modulus->2]` $=\{1\}$, hence amplitude $0$. On the *linear* (Clifford-sign, the $[\mathrm{HH}]$) fragment the Gröbner basis is literally Gaussian elimination (`GroebnerBasis[{x+y+z},{x,y,z},Modulus->2]` $=\{x+y+z\}$) and the point count is $2^{\,n-\mathrm{rank}}$ via `MatrixRank[\,\cdot\,, \text{Modulus}\to 2]`, polynomial and with no $2^n$ enumeration (rank $1$, count $4$, matching brute force on $\mathbb{F}_2^3$). On a *nonlinear* (magic) term, the amplitude point count is recovered without a path sum by the standard computer-algebra route: adjoin the field equations $x_i^2-x_i$, take the Gröbner basis, and count the standard monomials (the quotient-ring dimension). I verified $\#\{x: x_1x_2+x_3=0\}$ and $\#\{x: x_1x_2x_3+x_4=0\}$ agree exactly between this Gröbner quotient-dimension count and brute force, and that $\mathrm{gap}(f) = 2\,\#\{f=0\} - 2^n$.

**The honest scope limit.** This realization is clean only for the $\mathbb{F}_2$, real-amplitude (Toffoli-Hadamard) fragment, where the phase is $\mathbb{F}_2$-valued and "absorb the phase into an $\mathbb{F}_2$ ideal" is literally possible. The full Clifford+$T$ amplitude carries a $\mathbb{Z}_8$ phase, which is *not* a function into $\mathbb{F}_2$ and cannot be a single $\mathbb{F}_2$ ideal membership; recovering $\sum_v\omega^{\varphi(v)}$ would require stratifying by the eight values of $\varphi\bmod 8$ and counting each $\mathbb{Z}_8$ level set, and a level set $\varphi(v)\equiv c\pmod 8$ is a congruence, not an $\mathbb{F}_2$ polynomial equation, so it must be encoded by carry-propagating its binary digits, which blows the system up. This is the same wall the next candidate hits, and it is why Guan-Regan (`1904.00101/GuRe2019.tex`) can give a closed form for the *quadratic* (Clifford) Gauss sum over $\mathbb{Z}_4$ via $\mathbb{F}_2$ rank in matrix-multiplication time, but the *cubic* ($T$-rich) case stays $\#\mathrm{P}$-hard.

> **Verdict (a): a genuine but bounded realization, not a breakthrough.** The conceptual content (amplitude = variety point count) is Dawson 2004; the Gröbner-ideal-absorption technique is Amy-Stinchcombe-named future work that they themselves flag as no free lunch; a Wolfram Language version is the first concrete realization in the cited literature and is uniquely natural here, but it is genuinely useful only where the Gröbner basis is small (structured $\mathbb{F}_2$ varieties), it does not break the $\#\mathrm{P}$ wall, and it does not cleanly cover the $\mathbb{Z}_8$ $T$-rich case. It is a distinct algorithm with a distinct tractability parameter (ideal regularity, neither Amy's degree bound nor de Colnet's rank-width), and *that* distinctness is what the prior "future work" filing under-recorded. It is not a new tractable class.

### (b) The exact symbolic-parameter path sum

**The claim.** Carry the gate angle $\theta$ as a free symbol through the path sum and produce a closed-form amplitude, probability, or expectation *as a function of $\theta$* over an entire parametrized circuit family in one pass. For $H\,P(\theta)\,H$ with $P(\theta)=\mathrm{diag}(1,e^{i\theta})$ the path sum is $\langle x|HPH|0\rangle = \tfrac12\sum_{a\in\mathbb{F}_2} e^{i\theta a}(-1)^{ax}$, which I verified evaluates symbolically to the closed forms $(1+e^{i\theta})/2$ and $(1-e^{i\theta})/2$ (exactly the `StabilizerFrame` component coefficients), and the local expectation comes out in one symbolic pass as $\langle Z\rangle(\theta) = \cos\theta$. No re-traversal, no angle grid, no floating point.

**Does any competitor do this?** No. The grep of all thirteen papers for symbolic / free / parametric gate parameters is empty. `feynman` carries a symbolic phase polynomial but over *fixed* Clifford+$T$ gate phases, and in fact overflows its machine-integer dyadic arithmetic at 31 bits (`1805.06908/paper.tex`: "our implementation was able to verify implementations up to 31 bits in size, after which integer overflow occurs due to our handling of dyadic arithmetic"); `quizx` and the stabilizer-rank tools and de Colnet are numeric coefficient-ring engines; Koh-Penney-Spekkens evaluate numeric Weil sums; SymPhase symbolizes only the *measurement-outcome sign bits*, not gate angles. This capability is structurally absent from every cited tool because none is hosted in a computer-algebra system.

**Is it novel, or just a convenience?** It is not a new complexity result: off the Clifford-foldable / low-residual fragment the closed form has $2^{\text{residual}}$ terms in $\theta$ and inherits the $\#\mathrm{P}$ wall, so "amplitude as a function of $\theta$" is only as compact as the structure allows. But within the structured fragment it is a capability that produces objects the competitors cannot produce at all: an exact $\langle H\rangle(\vec\theta)$ over a VQE or QAOA ansatz family, hence exact gradients and exact landscape geometry, computed once rather than sampled on a grid. Calling this "implementation" undersells it; it is the cleanest defensible sense in which the Wolfram Language host enables something no other listed simulator can.

**The honest limit I verified.** `RootReduce` canonicalizes only *parameter-free* algebraic numbers, so two symbolic-$\theta$ amplitudes cannot in general be machine-decided equal by `RootReduce`; one falls back to `FullSimplify` with assumptions, which is heuristic. So the exactness is real but the *decision* of symbolic-parameter equality is not free.

> **Verdict (b): a genuine, WL-unique capability, under-weighted by the prior note.** No cited simulator carries free gate parameters; all are numeric. Exact symbolic-parameter path sums are therefore a capability difference, not a speed ratio, and are the strongest defensible "uniquely WL-enabled" contribution. They are not a new tractable class (they inherit the wall off the structured fragment), and symbolic-parameter equality testing is heuristic, but the prior note's "implementation" tag was the wrong altitude for this one item.

### (c) The $GF(2^k)$ trace / character-sum reformulation

**The claim, and why it fails.** The mapping's headline #1 proposes generalizing the simulator from $\mathbb{F}_2$ to $GF(2^k)$ via the additive character $\chi(x)=(-1)^{\mathrm{Tr}(x)}$, which `FiniteFieldElementTrace` gives natively. The trace is genuinely the right object for an $\mathbb{F}_2$-*linear* character sum, and I verified the balance $\sum_{x\in GF(2^4)}(-1)^{\mathrm{Tr}(x)}=0$. But the additive character of $GF(2^k)$ is $\mathbb{Z}_2$-valued, so it can only encode the *linear, sign* part of the phase, which is already handled by linear algebra. It cannot see the $\mathbb{Z}_8$ magic phase, and the obstruction is exactly the one Koh-Penney-Spekkens hit. They evaluate Clifford amplitudes in closed form by diagonalizing a quadratic form over $\mathbb{F}_p$ and summing Weil sums, *for $p$ an odd prime only*, and they state precisely why qubits are excluded (`1702.03316/aUb.tex`): "it is **evident that we cannot find a polynomial $S_P(s,t)$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$** such that the matrix elements ... are of the form $\chi(S_P(s,t))$. ... one needs to **define the phase polynomial to be over $\mathbb{Z}_4$ instead**. ... [the diagonalization theorem] works **only for fields that are not of characteristic 2**." The phase lives in $\mathbb{Z}_4$ or $\mathbb{Z}_8$, not in any $GF(2^k)$.

**Kernel check (verified).** The characteristic-2 wall is concrete. A primitive eighth-root phase cannot sit in a Pauli sign: $(e^{i\pi/4}X)^2 = iI \neq I$, with eigenvalues $\pm e^{i\pi/4}$ (no $+1$ eigenvalue), so $e^{i\pi/4}X$ stabilizes no state. And the cyclotomic structure of $\omega$ collapses entirely modulo $2$: $\mathrm{Cyclotomic}[8,x]=1+x^4$ factors over $\mathbb{F}_2$ as $(1+x)^4$. The ring $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ that carries the magic has no faithful image in characteristic $2$.

> **Verdict (c): elegant restatement, no new capability.** The $GF(2^k)$ trace is the correct native form of the additive character and a clean way to write the linear/sign fragment, but the genuine field-theoretic Gauss-sum win is Koh-Penney-Spekkens', and it is odd-characteristic only. For qubit Clifford+$T$ (characteristic $2$, $\mathbb{Z}_8$ phase) the trace formulation buys nothing the linear-algebra fold does not already give. Not novel, not useful here.

### (d) Other directions the pages suggest

**Wolfram Language as the native rewriter.** Amy's $[\mathrm{HH}]/[\omega]/[\mathrm{Elim}]$ rules and Amy-Stinchcombe's confluent system are a term-rewriting calculus on symbolic sums. In Wolfram Language `Inactive[Sum]` + `HoldPattern` rules + `Activate`, with the path-sum head marked `HoldAll` and `NonThreadable`, *is* that calculus, expressed in the language rather than implemented inside a Haskell program. The docs confirm the inert sum can be differentiated and rewritten without expansion and is faster than `Sum` for that reason. This is a real ergonomic and prototyping advantage: confluence experiments, alternative rule sets, and the "carry the sum, do not enumerate" discipline are one-liners, and the build already uses it (the `PathSum`PS` head, the shuffled-order confluence replay). But it is an advantage in *expressing and exploring* the method, not a new method: it does not change which circuits are tractable.

**Resolve / quantifier elimination.** `Resolve[Exists[v, constraints], Booleans]` and the `FiniteField` domain decide satisfiability and *project out* a variable, which is the support/consistency side of the variety story, but projection is not a weighted count, so it cannot evaluate the amplitude any more than `GroebnerBasis` alone can.

**Holonomic / generating-function evaluation.** The `Inactive[Sum]` docs show a parametric-differentiation trick (differentiate $\sum_k e^{a k^2}$ to get $\mathrm{EllipticTheta}$ relations), and `RootSum` sums a fixed form over the roots of one polynomial in closed form. Neither reaches the path sum. I verified the honest limit the mapping already noted: `RootSum[1+\#^4\&, \#\&]` evaluates to $0$ (a Vieta/linear root-of-unity sum), whereas the quadratic Gauss sum $\sum_k e^{i\pi k^2/n}$ has closed forms ($n=2\to 1+i$, $n=4\to 2e^{i\pi/4}$, $n=6\to (1+i)\sqrt3$) that are *not* of the form `RootSum[onePolynomial, oneForm]`, because the exponent $k\mapsto k^2$ is not a single fixed function over the roots of a single polynomial. The generating-function route does not give the Gauss sum.

> **Verdict (d): ergonomic and expository wins, no complexity novelty.** Wolfram Language is an unusually natural host for symbolic-sum rewriting, and that has genuine value for prototyping and pedagogy, but it does not enlarge the tractable class. `Resolve` gives projection/consistency, not counts; the holonomic/`RootSum` route does not reach the quadratic Gauss sum.

## 4. Why the prior analysis read the way it did (the diagnosis, sharpened)

Putting Sections 2 and 3 together: the prior verdict was not careless, it was correctly answering a narrower question. It asked "does the Wolfram Language work contain a new *method* (a new tractable class, a new complexity result)?" and the answer is genuinely no. The framing made two real findings invisible *as findings*:

1. The survey's organizing axis was "which primitive implements step X of the existing pipeline." Under that axis, `GroebnerBasis[Modulus->2]` reads as "replace the hand-rolled Boolean reduction," an implementation swap, and the variety generalization is recorded as an aside ("this is *also* the literal realization of the report's named generalization"). The distinctness of the Gröbner point count *as an algorithm with its own tractability parameter*, separate from Amy's degree bound and de Colnet's rank-width, never gets weighed.

2. The benchmark axis was numeric timing (dense `Method -> "Schrodinger"` versus the engine). On a numeric axis, "symbolic angles" can only show up as "the dense path produced no closed form," which the note duly recorded (Section 4.3) but then tagged "implementation" in its contribution table, because the table's column was "method vs implementation," not "capability vs no-capability." A capability that *no competitor possesses* does not fit a method-vs-implementation table, so it landed in the wrong cell.

The fix is not to overturn the verdict. It is to add one row the table was missing: *capability not present in any competing tool*. Symbolic-parameter path sums belong in that row; the Gröbner-variety point count belongs in a second new row, *concrete realization of a named-open problem with a distinct tractability parameter*. Neither row is "a new method that breaks the wall," which is why the harsh half of the verdict survives.

Every Wolfram Language claim above was reproduced in a clean kernel by `novelty-demos.wls` (this folder), run with `wolframscript`, no `Quiet`, no `Print`: the Nullstellensatz amplitude-zero test, the linear-fragment point count via `MatrixRank` matching brute force, the Gröbner quotient-dimension point count matching brute force for a quadratic and a cubic $\mathbb{F}_2$ term with $\mathrm{gap}=2\#\{f=0\}-2^n$, the symbolic-$\theta$ amplitudes $(1\pm e^{i\theta})/2$ and $\langle Z\rangle(\theta)=\cos\theta$ matching the `StabilizerFrame` coefficients, the trace balance $\sum_{GF(2^4)}(-1)^{\mathrm{Tr}}=0$, the characteristic-2 wall $(e^{i\pi/4}X)^2=iI\neq I$ with eigenvalues $\pm e^{i\pi/4}$ and $1+x^4\equiv(1+x)^4\pmod 2$, and the `RootSum`-versus-quadratic-Gauss-sum limit. Every literature claim is anchored to the fetched arXiv TeX by file and line: Dawson's "counting the points in an algebraic variety" (`quant-ph_0408129`), Montanaro's $\mathrm{gap}(f)$ and the degree-2 / degree-3 dichotomy (`1607.08473`), Koh-Penney-Spekkens' characteristic-2 exclusion (`1702.03316`), Guan-Regan's $\mathbb{Z}_4$ quadratic-form rank result (`1904.00101`), Amy's 31-bit overflow (`1805.06908`), Amy-Stinchcombe's "offloads the difficulty to ... Gröbner bases and counting points in varieties" future-work passage (`2408.02778/sections/conclusion.tex`), and de Colnet's rank-width fixed-parameter dynamic program citing Dawson (`2605.29944`).

## 6. Conclusion and the answer to the question

**Was a genuinely novel WL-enabled approach missed?** Not in the sense the user feared, and partly in a sense the user is right about.

There is **no** novel approach that breaks the $\#\mathrm{P}$ wall or enlarges the efficiently simulable class. The path-sum object, the degree bound, the rewrite rules, the residual completion, the hidden shift, the variety-point-count *idea* (Dawson 2004), and the rank-width fixed-parameter evaluation (de Colnet 2026) are all prior art, and the prior note's "no new mathematics" is correct.

But the prior verdict's flat "implementation only" undersold two real things, both consequences of hosting the path sum in a computer-algebra system rather than a numeric one. First, **exact symbolic-parameter path sums are a capability that none of the cited simulators has**, because all of them are built on fixed numeric coefficient rings; producing a closed-form amplitude or expectation *function* over a parametrized circuit family in one pass is a capability difference, not a convenience, even though it inherits the wall off the structured fragment and its equality testing is heuristic. Second, **the Gröbner-basis / variety point count is the first concrete realization, in the cited literature, of a technique Amy-Stinchcombe explicitly leave open**, distinct in method and in tractability parameter from every competitor, uniquely natural in Wolfram Language, but bounded to the $\mathbb{F}_2$ real-amplitude fragment and, by the originators' own statement, no asymptotic free lunch. The other two directions, the $GF(2^k)$ character sum and the holonomic/generating-function route, are dead ends blocked by a clean characteristic-2 obstruction.

So the honest re-weighting is: the prior note's verdict stands as a statement about *methods*, and should gain two sentences about *capabilities*. If a single line of novelty is wanted, it is this: **the one thing the Wolfram Language host does that no competing Clifford+$T$ simulator does is carry the gate parameters as free symbols and return the amplitude as an exact function of them; everything else is a faithful, and unusually comfortable, re-expression of methods that already exist.**

## A note for a physicist

The object in play is a Feynman amplitude written as a finite Gauss sum $\sum_v \omega^{\varphi(v)}$, $\omega=e^{i\pi/4}$, where the path variables $v$ are bits and $\varphi$ is a low-degree pseudo-Boolean phase valued in $\mathbb{Z}_8$. For real-valued (Toffoli-Hadamard) circuits this collapses to a *signed count of the zeros of a Boolean polynomial*, that is, the number of points of an algebraic variety over the two-element field, an object number theorists have studied since long before quantum computing. The interesting physics-adjacent question was whether Wolfram Language's symbolic algebra unlocks a genuinely new way to evaluate that count. The answer is that it does not change *what is hard*: the magic ($T$-gate) content sits in the cubic part of $\varphi$, whose weighted point count is $\#\mathrm{P}$-hard, and no amount of Gröbner-basis machinery or finite-field trace algebra makes that polynomial, because the characteristic-2 field cannot even *hold* the eighth-root-of-unity phase ($e^{i\pi/4}X$ squares to $iI$, not $I$; the cyclotomic polynomial $1+x^4$ degenerates to $(1+x)^4$ modulo $2$). What the symbolic host *does* uniquely give is the freedom to leave a gate's rotation angle as an unbound symbol and read off the amplitude as a closed-form function of it, the way one would carry a coupling constant through a calculation by hand rather than re-running it at a grid of numerical values. That is a real and, in this corner, unique convenience of working inside a computer-algebra system, but it is a convenience of *representation*, not a loophole in the complexity of the sum.

The deliverable is one Markdown file, `WL-Symbolic-Novelty-Reexamination.md`, plus the verification script `novelty-demos.wls`; no kernel code was changed.
